Why it matters

Relevance of Technical Drawing

Engineering drawings are not just sketches — they are legally binding, internationally understood instructions that bridge the gap between imagination and reality.

📐

Universal Language

A technical drawing created in India can be manufactured in Germany, assembled in Japan, and installed in Brazil — without a single word being translated. Geometry is universal.

🔩

Precise Instructions

Every dimension, every tolerance, every surface finish specification in a drawing tells a machinist or builder exactly what to make. Ambiguity costs money and lives in engineering.

🏗️

Foundation of All Engineering

Whether you design bridges, microchips, aircraft, or medical devices — technical drawing is the first step. Every great structure existed first as lines on paper.

Types of Lines

In engineering drawing, every type of line has a specific meaning. Using the wrong line type is like using the wrong word — it changes the meaning entirely.

Type Line Name Used For
Type A Continuous Thick Visible outlines and edges of the object
Type B Continuous Thin Dimension lines, extension lines, projection lines, leader lines
Type E/F Dashed Medium Hidden outlines and edges not visible from that view
Type G Chain Thin Centre lines, axes of symmetry, pitch circles, trajectories
Type C Continuous Thin Freehand Short break lines showing cut sections
Measurement Standards

Dimensioning Techniques

Dimensioning tells the manufacturer the exact size, location, and form of every feature. A drawing without proper dimensions is useless.

01

Elements of a Dimension

Every dimension has three parts: the dimension line (with arrowheads), extension lines (extending ~3mm beyond), and the dimension figure (the number).

02

Aligned System

Dimensions are placed perpendicular to the dimension line and readable from the bottom or right-hand edge of the sheet. Most common in Indian practice.

03

Unidirectional System

All dimensions are oriented to be read from the bottom of the drawing. Preferred in modern CAD environments.

04

Key Rules

Dimensions in millimetres (mm). Never repeat a dimension. Keep dimension lines at least 8 mm away from object outlines.

Projection of Points

The Four Quadrants

When HP and VP intersect, they create four dihedral angles called quadrants. The position of a point determines which quadrant it occupies and how its projections appear in the 2D drawing.

I

First Quadrant

Above HP · In front of VP

The most common quadrant used in practice (First Angle Projection — Indian standard).

p' above xy p below xy
II

Second Quadrant

Above HP · Behind VP

Both views overlap above the XY line. The drawing can become ambiguous, so this quadrant is rarely used in practice.

p' above xy p above xy
III

Third Quadrant

Below HP · Behind VP

Used in Third Angle Projection (American standard). Views are opposite to First Angle.

p' below xy p above xy
IV

Fourth Quadrant

Below HP · In front of VP

Both views overlap below the XY line, causing ambiguity. Not used in standard practice.

p' below xy p below xy
Indian Standard

BIS Code of Practice

The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) publishes SP 46:2003 — the definitive guide for engineering drawing practice in India. Every drawing in Indian industry must follow these rules.

SP 46:2003

Engineering Drawing Practice for Schools and Colleges — establishes uniform standards for line types, dimensioning, projection methods, and drawing sheet formats across India.

First Angle Projection

India follows First Angle (European) projection. The object is placed between the observer and the projection plane. Views are placed: front view centre, top view below, right side view to the left.

Why Standards Matter

Without standards, a drawing made in Chennai might be misread by a workshop in Pune. BIS ensures every engineer speaks the same visual language, reducing costly errors in manufacturing.
